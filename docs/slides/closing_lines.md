# Closing Lines — I³ Interview

This file is the verbatim reference for the final 60 seconds of the
presentation and the candidate-questions segment that follows.

---

## 1. Verbatim Closing Line (Slide 15)

Deliver exactly as written. No paraphrasing. No preamble.

> *"I build intelligent systems that adapt to people.
> I'd like to do that in your lab."*

**Delivery notes:**

- Pause a full second after the previous slide's last point before starting.
- Two beats between the first sentence and the second — not a rushed comma.
- Hold a three-second silence after the final word. Do not fill it. The
  silence is the performance.
- Then move to the verbal thank-you on the next line.

**Thank-you line (immediately after the silence):**

> *"Thank you. Happy to take questions — and when we get to candidate
> questions at the end, I have three prepared."*

## 2. Verbatim "What This Prototype Is *Not*" Wording (Slide 13)

Title is literal: **"What This Prototype Is *Not*"**. Use exactly that
phrase on the slide. Six bullets in the order below — the ordering is
load-bearing because the accessibility bullet is the Matthew-values moment
and must come fourth, not first or last, so it lands in the middle of the
slide's cognitive weight.

1. **Not a shipped product.** A 17-day prototype, on one laptop.
2. **Not trained on real user data** — synthetic archetypes from HCI literature (Epp 2011, Vizer 2009, Zimmermann 2014).
3. **Not a strong SLM** — 8 M params, limited by synthetic dialogue training.
4. **Not universal accessibility detection** — keystroke-only misses screen-reader, voice-control, gaze users. Adaptation must stay opt-out capable.
5. **Not zero-information embeddings** — lossy, abstract, still identity-signalling.
6. **Not multi-modal yet** — keystroke only; the TCN itself is modality-agnostic.

**Delivery note on bullet 4:** make eye contact with Matthew for the
duration of this bullet. It is the deliberate acknowledgement of his
TextSpaced work and Apple accessibility lineage — restraint and honesty
matter more than flourish.

## 3. Three Candidate Questions — Primary Selections

Pick three from the full set below depending on how the interview has
gone. These are the three **default primaries** if no contextual reason
overrides them. All six are available; choose based on what the panel
has already revealed.

### Primary 1 — Matthew's Apple-to-Huawei lineage

> *"You spent a decade at Apple working on things like Fall Detection and
> Crash Detection — features that infer user state from implicit signals.
> What drew you to bringing that philosophy to Huawei, and how does the
> London lab differ from what you were doing before?"*

**Why this primary:** It shows you have done homework without being creepy,
it lets Matthew talk about his own work (flattering, honest), and it
reveals his view of Huawei vs. Apple without asking pointed questions.
Deploy this first — it is the warmest opener.

### Primary 2 — Evaluation framework

> *"When you evaluate a prototype from an AI/ML specialist, what separates
> a good one from a great one in your mind?"*

**Why this primary:** Direct but not aggressive. His answer is the
evaluation framework he just used on you — which gives you something
useful to reflect on afterward and signals that you want to keep improving.

### Primary 3 — Edinburgh Joint Lab

> *"How does the lab work with the Edinburgh Joint Lab — do concepts flow
> from their research into your prototyping?"*

**Why this primary:** Shows you know the Edinburgh Joint Lab exists,
references the March 2026 Nissim talk indirectly, reveals organisational
structure. This is the "I understand the pipeline" question.

## 4. Alternative Candidate Questions — Ready To Swap In

If the conversation has already covered any of the primaries, or if the
panel's tone suggests a different question would land better, swap in
from here. Three alternatives — any of them is deployable.

### Alternative A — Balance between shipping and research

> *"The HMI Lab is newly established. What's the balance between building
> things that ship into products versus things that stay as patents or
> research publications?"*

**Use when:** the panel has emphasised concept-to-prototype work or hinted
that publications are not the only goal. Reveals the realistic impact path.

### Alternative B — Shenzhen collaboration rhythm

> *"What's the collaboration rhythm with Shenzhen HQ? How much of the
> lab's work is responding to their briefs versus generating new concepts?"*

**Use when:** the panel has discussed Huawei's broader organisation or
mentioned specific product lines from Shenzhen. Reveals the day-to-day
working dynamics — what the actual job looks like.

### Alternative C — Under-explored HMI problems

> *"Is there a type of HMI problem you feel the field underestimates —
> something you'd really like to see someone work on?"*

**Use when:** the conversation has been heavily technical and you want
to end on a forward-looking, creative note. His answer is effectively
the real job spec — what you'd actually work on.
