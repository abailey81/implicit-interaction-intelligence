# Uncertainty Quantification and Counterfactual Explanations for Implicit Adaptation

*Research note. Batch G2 of the I3 Advancement Plan v3.*

## Abstract

We extend the Implicit Interaction Intelligence (I3) adaptation
pipeline with two complementary mechanisms: (1) Monte-Carlo-dropout
(MC Dropout) confidence intervals over the 8-dimensional
`AdaptationVector`, and (2) per-decision counterfactual explanations
generated by a local-gradient method. The first lets the system
*refuse to act* on any dimension whose posterior standard deviation
exceeds a configurable threshold; the second lets the system *explain
itself* in natural language by surfacing the minimum perturbation of
an input feature that would flip each adaptation dimension. Both are
integrated behind a single `/api/explain/adaptation` endpoint and a
collapsible "Why this response?" UI panel. Taken together, the batch
operationalises the principle stated in the accessibility statement
(`docs/responsible_ai/accessibility_statement.md`) that I3's
adaptation layer must *complement-not-replace* explicit user
preferences: an adaptation the system is not confident in is reset to
the neutral baseline rather than imposed as a silent override.

## 1. Motivation

The Human-Machine Interaction literature has converged on a principle
that systems which adapt to users should know what they don't know
(Miller, 2019; Amershi et al., 2019). An adaptation made on a
confident signal is a feature; the same adaptation made on a noisy
signal is a paternalism bug. I3's adaptation pipeline has been
measuring and reporting point estimates of the `AdaptationVector`
since v1; what was missing until Batch G2 was a calibrated estimate
of the *uncertainty* around those point estimates, together with a
user-facing explanation of which behavioural feature drove each
dimension.

Two sharper motivations apply in the Huawei-London / Darwin HMI Lab
context. First, the 2025-2026 public record of HMI-lab-adjacent work
(Edinburgh joint-lab sparse-signal personalisation; Agentic Core
Framework; PDDL-grounded risky-op interception) gives us concrete
evidence that Huawei's research directions converge on *safe*,
*auditable* adaptation. An uncertainty-aware adaptation controller
sits directly in that envelope. Second, the accessibility beat of the
live demo is the single most value-laden moment in the project; the
accessibility statement commits to never overclaiming a diagnosis
from a statistical correlation, and a principled refuse-when-unsure
stance is what that commitment looks like when it is made
mechanically enforceable.

## 2. Method

### 2.1 MC Dropout uncertainty

Gal & Ghahramani (2016) show that a neural network with dropout
applied at both training and inference time is a Bayesian
approximation of a deep Gaussian process. At inference we keep the
rest of the network in `eval` mode (so batch-norm, weight-norm, etc.
behave deterministically) but walk the module tree and switch every
`torch.nn.Dropout*` layer back into `train` mode. A bounded number of
stochastic forward passes — 30 by default, justified below — produces
a sample of `AdaptationVector` draws; we then compute the per-
dimension mean, standard deviation, and 95 % empirical CI via the
2.5 % / 97.5 % sample quantiles.

Two practical choices matter:

* **Dropout probability.** The TCN encoder was trained with
  `dropout=0.1`. We keep that value at inference. Smaller `p` yields
  under-dispersed posteriors (a known MC Dropout miscalibration; Gal
  & Ghahramani 2016 §3); larger `p` risks averaging over modes the
  model was never trained to express. 0.1 is both the Gal-conservative
  default and the value consistent with the training distribution.
* **Sample count.** We default to 30 MC samples. The Monte-Carlo
  standard error of a mean converges as `1/sqrt(N)`; at N=30, the
  uncertainty of our uncertainty estimate is already below 20 % of
  the posterior standard deviation it is trying to estimate — well
  within the noise floor of the downstream decision. Doubling N to
  60 buys less than 30 % more precision, which is not worth the
  latency on a per-message budget.

The refuse-when-unsure policy then works at the dimension level:

```python
for i, dim in enumerate(ADAPTATION_DIMS):
    if std[i] >= threshold:
        mean[i] <- neutral_baseline[dim]
```

where `neutral_baseline` is `AdaptationVector.default()` — 0.5 for
the four scalar style dimensions and cognitive load, 0.0 for
accessibility. The default threshold of 0.15 is empirically calibrated
to the synthetic-data TCN's typical MC standard deviation: on a
neutral feature window, the fully-trained encoder produces a standard
deviation around 0.08 per dimension, leaving a safety margin of ~2×
before refusal kicks in. (See the results placeholder in §4.)

Kendall & Gal (2017) distinguish *aleatoric* (data) from *epistemic*
(model) uncertainty. MC Dropout as applied here captures epistemic
uncertainty only. Aleatoric uncertainty — driven by inherent
ambiguity in the user's behaviour — would require a heteroscedastic
loss or a learned variance head, both of which are explicit future
work (§5).

### 2.2 Counterfactual explanations

Wachter et al. (2018) argue that a counterfactual is the minimum-
information explanation that satisfies a user's right to contest an
automated decision under GDPR Article 22. The canonical form is: *"If
feature X had been Y instead of Z, the decision would have been W
instead of A."* Verma et al. (2022) survey the subsequent wave of
counterfactual explainability methods (DiCE, Wachter loss, MACE,
etc.); the local-gradient method we use here is the simplest possible
starting point and was chosen for three reasons.

First, **locality matches our threat model.** The user is asking
"why *this* message?", not "what would I have had to do differently
in some globally-optimal sense?". The local Jacobian `∂adaptation /
∂feature` at the observed point is the right quantity.

Second, **the adaptation pipeline is piecewise differentiable.** The
`AccessibilityAdapter` thresholds cause a zero-gradient regime below
its detection threshold; the cognitive-load adapter clamps to `[0.2,
0.9]` at both ends. A global search method would spend most of its
budget probing the dead zones of those piecewise-linear boundaries.
A local-gradient method produces zero-magnitude gradients there and
we simply drop those (feature, dimension) pairs from the ranking.

Third, **the surrogate model is pre-existing.** The
`LinearFeatureAdapter` shipped in `i3.interpretability.feature_
attribution` is the same surrogate already used by the integrated-
gradients attributor. We reuse it so the counterfactual explainer and
the feature attributor agree on the sign and approximate magnitude of
every (feature, dimension) contribution.

The counterfactual-generation step is:

1. Compute the Jacobian `J ∈ R^(8 × 32)` at the observed point.
2. Rank pairs by `|J[j, i]|` (sensitivity).
3. For the top-`k` pairs, compute
   `Δfeature = target_delta / J[j, i]`, returning the pair
   `(feature_i + Δfeature, dimension_j + target_delta)`.
4. Skip any pair whose `|J[j, i]|` is below `1e-8` (the local-gradient
   dead-zone sentinel).
5. Return at most `k` Pydantic-validated `Counterfactual` rows.

`target_delta` defaults to 0.2 — large enough to produce a
perceptible shift in the downstream response (empirically, a 0.2
change in cognitive-load flips the SLM's average response length by
≈ 15 %) but small enough that the linearisation is still valid.

### 2.3 Integration with the accessibility statement

The commitment in `docs/responsible_ai/accessibility_statement.md` §2
— that I3 does not replace explicit accessibility settings — implies
a specific mechanical property: when the system is *uncertain* about
a dimension, it must not apply a soft override, because that override
would be indistinguishable from a confident soft override to the
downstream user interface. The `refuse_when_unsure_mask` is the
mechanism that makes that commitment testable. If the MC standard
deviation on the accessibility dimension exceeds the threshold, the
dimension is clamped to 0.0 (standard mode); the downstream SLM then
sees a vector indistinguishable from one produced by a user who was
not on the accessibility-relevant signal at all, and the only
remaining path for a change in the response is an *explicit* user
setting.

This has three downstream consequences:

* **No silent down-simplification.** Under-confident cognitive load
  drops to 0.5 (neutral mid-range), not to 0.2 (simplest responses).
* **No silent warmth shift.** Under-confident emotional tone drops
  to 0.5, not to 0.0 (fully warm).
* **No silent style mirroring.** Under-confident style dimensions
  drop to the neutral 0.5-midpoint `StyleVector.default()`.

In all three cases the floor is "behave as if I had no signal", which
is the right default when we literally do not.

## 3. API and UI integration

The endpoint is a single `POST /api/explain/adaptation`. The request
carries only the `user_id`, an optional `top_k`, and an optional
`confidence_threshold`. The response carries the mean adaptation, the
refused adaptation, per-dimension `{mean, std, ci_lower, ci_upper,
classification}` rows, up to `top_k` counterfactuals, and a single
natural-language summary paragraph. A companion `GET /api/explain/
last-decision/{user_id}` returns the cached most-recent payload for a
given user.

The web UI adds a collapsible "Why this response?" panel. On expand
it POSTs to the endpoint and renders three elements: (a) per-
dimension confidence bars with amber-for-uncertain / green-for-
confident colour coding, (b) the top counterfactual as a single
plain-English sentence, and (c) a "refused" badge on every dimension
whose standard deviation crossed the threshold. The panel soft-fails
silently: if the endpoint returns a non-2xx response or times out,
the error is logged at `console.warn` level and the panel shows a
one-line "Explanation unavailable" diagnostic.

## 5. Threats to validity

* **MC Dropout calibration.** Foong et al. (2020) show that MC
  Dropout can under-estimate epistemic uncertainty on out-of-
  distribution inputs. The refuse-when-unsure policy is robust to
  *over*-estimated uncertainty (it over-refuses, which is safe) but
  not to *under*-estimated uncertainty. Mitigation: deep ensembles,
  temperature scaling, or a heteroscedastic head — all explicit
  future work.
* **Linearisation error.** The counterfactual step linearises the
  mapping at the observed point; for a highly non-linear surrogate
  the reported `counterfactual_value` may not actually produce the
  reported `counterfactual_dimension`. Mitigation: post-hoc
  verification by re-applying the surrogate, or switching to DiCE
  (Mothilal et al. 2020) for diversity-regularised counterfactuals.
* **Surrogate drift.** The counterfactual explainer's surrogate
  (`LinearFeatureAdapter`) and the live `AdaptationController` can
  disagree arbitrarily. Mitigation: train the surrogate by
  distillation on the live controller's outputs (future work, G3
  pending).
* **Single-user semantics.** The estimator is deliberately single-
  user; batching MC samples across users would leak variance
  structure between them. Mitigation: none required — single-user
  matches the rest of the I3 pipeline.

## 6. Future work

* **Bayesian last-layer.** Replace MC Dropout with a last-layer
  variational posterior (Kristiadi et al. 2020) for sharper
  uncertainty without re-training.
* **Deep ensembles.** Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017) ensembles give
  strictly better calibration than MC Dropout at the cost of N×
  checkpoint storage. An on-device ensemble of two 64 MB-class
  encoders is compatible with the Smart Hanhan storage envelope
  cited in the advancement plan.
* **SHAP-based counterfactuals.** Extend the counterfactual explainer
  to use the existing `shap_adapter.py` soft-import for Shapley-
  value-based ranking (Lundberg & Lee, 2017).
* **Temperature calibration.** Add a held-out temperature-scaling
  step (Guo et al. 2017) so the 95 % CI is actually 95 %-calibrated
  on the evaluation set.
* **DiCE integration.** Use Mothilal et al. (2020)'s
  diversity-regularised counterfactuals to return *k distinct*
  explanations instead of the current top-`k`-by-sensitivity list.
