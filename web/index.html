Privacy

Your data, your device.

Everything happens on this machine. No request leaves the host while the cloud route is off. Embeddings persisted to disk are Fernet-encrypted; raw text is never written.

On-device

No cloud round-trip

The default response path runs the custom SLM locally via PyTorch with the cloud client gated behind an explicit flag.

Encryption at rest

Fernet · AES-128-CBC + HMAC

Persisted state embeddings are encrypted with a Fernet key derived from the host's secrets. The DB schema has no column for raw text.

PII sanitiser

Regex + rule-based redaction

Email, phone, IP, URL, credit-card, and SSN-like patterns are scrubbed from any text before it reaches the diary or a future cloud handler.

Origin allow-list

CORS doesn't cover WebSockets

The WebSocket handler enforces an explicit Origin allow-list because the browser CORS layer doesn't apply to upgrade requests.

Privacy budget · cloud route

Hard upper bounds enforced per session: 50 cloud calls and 1 MB transmitted. The user's consent flag is required; the per-prompt complexity score and the LinUCB bandit decide whether the cloud arm fires inside that envelope.

    Design Decisions

    Thirteen choices, thirteen reasons.

    For every architectural decision in the system, what we chose, what else we considered, and why this option won. Each card cites the paper or reference that informed it.

    1. TCN over RNN/LSTM for the keystroke encoder

    DecisionDilated temporal convolutional network with residual blocks and metric-loss training.
    AlternativesLSTM, GRU, plain 1D conv, transformer encoder.
    WhyParallel training over the full window (no recurrent unrolling), deterministic fixed receptive field, exports cleanly to ONNX, runs at 3.7 ms p50 on CPU. RNNs introduce hidden-state drift that hurts the metric-learning objective.
    ReferenceBai, Kolter & Koltun (2018) — An Empirical Evaluation of Generic Convolutional and Recurrent Networks for Sequence Modeling.

    2. MoE-FFN over standard feed-forward

    DecisionTwo-expert mixture-of-experts feed-forward with a softmax gate conditioned on the 8-dim adaptation vector.
    AlternativesStandard GELU FFN, gated linear units (GLU/SwiGLU), per-layer adapter modules.
    WhyCapacity per parameter: 2 experts give specialisation under the adaptation conditioning while staying within the 6 GB GPU training budget. The gating output is a free interpretability signal.
    ReferenceShazeer et al. (2017) — Outrageously Large Neural Networks: The Sparsely-Gated Mixture-of-Experts Layer.

    3. ACT halting over fixed depth

    DecisionPer-token Adaptive Computation Time controller that freezes tokens via a halting mask once their cumulative halting probability exceeds a threshold.
    AlternativesFixed 12-layer depth for every token, early-exit classifiers, layer-skip routing.
    WhyAdapts compute to user state: tokens that converge early under a low-cognitive-load adaptation can halt in 4–6 layers; complex prompts use the full stack. A ponder loss prevents pathological non-halting.
    ReferenceGraves (2016) — Adaptive Computation Time for Recurrent Neural Networks.

    4. Per-layer cross-attention over pooled prompt prefix

    DecisionCross-attention head at every transformer layer, attending the residual stream onto the user-state & adaptation conditioning sequence.
    AlternativesPool the conditioning into a single prefix token, prepend to the prompt, FiLM-style scale&shift biases.
    WhyPooled conditioning gets diluted as the residual stream evolves; per-layer cross-attention re-injects the adaptation at every depth, so token-by-token style steering is mechanically possible. Ablation confirms: zero this head and adaptation faithfulness collapses.
    ReferenceVaswani et al. (2017) — Attention Is All You Need; Perez et al. (2018) — FiLM: Visual Reasoning with a General Conditioning Layer (which we evaluated and rejected).

    5. Byte-level BPE over WordPiece / SentencePiece

    DecisionByte-level Byte-Pair Encoding, 32 k vocab, hand-implemented in 217 LOC of pure Python.
    AlternativesWordPiece (BERT), SentencePiece unigram, the tokenizers Rust crate, character-level.
    WhyByte-level guarantees no UNK fallback ever — every Unicode codepoint is reachable. Implementation-from-scratch is the demonstration: the entire tokenizer is one readable Python file with no external runtime dependency.
    ReferenceSennrich, Haddow & Birch (2015) — Neural Machine Translation of Rare Words with Subword Units; Radford et al. (2019) — Language Models are Unsupervised Multitask Learners (GPT-2 byte-level preprocessing).

    6. LoRA over full fine-tuning for personalisation

    DecisionLow-rank adapters (rank 8) on the attention projections, one adapter per biometric template.
    AlternativesFull fine-tuning per user, prefix tuning, IA3, prompt tuning.
    WhyPer-user state is ~1–2 MB instead of 200 MB. Parameter efficiency means fewer training samples needed for personalisation to take effect. The frozen backbone is shared, so all users benefit from the same well-trained weights with only the delta personalised.
    ReferenceHu et al. (2021) — LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models; Houlsby et al. (2019) — Parameter-Efficient Transfer Learning for NLP.

    7. Frozen MobileNetV3 backbone + new gaze head

    DecisionUse MobileNetV3 as a frozen visual feature extractor; train a small calibration head on 4 target points per session.
    AlternativesEnd-to-end fine-tuning the backbone, training a CNN from scratch, a vision transformer.
    WhyProtects the pretrained features that took millions of images to learn; per-session calibration is cheap (~2 seconds, 4 fixations) and the only thing that personalises. The backbone runs in-tab via onnxruntime-web — camera frames never leave the browser.
    ReferenceHoward et al. (2019) — Searching for MobileNetV3.

    8. LinUCB over Thompson sampling for routing

    DecisionLinear Upper Confidence Bound contextual bandit over a 12-dim feature vector for the {edge SLM, cloud LLM} arms.
    AlternativesContextual Thompson sampling, ε-greedy, hand-tuned threshold, neural bandit.
    WhyLinUCB has an analytical confidence interval, so the cloud threshold is a clean dashed line on the routing scatter and easy to debug. Thompson sampling needs posterior approximation; the convergence properties at our small batch size are noisier in practice.
    ReferenceLi, Chu, Langford & Schapire (2010) — A Contextual-Bandit Approach to Personalized News Article Recommendation.

    9. Cosine + BM25 reranking over pure cosine

    DecisionHybrid retrieval: cosine similarity over dense embeddings recalls the top-k=50, BM25 reranks to top-3.
    AlternativesPure cosine, pure BM25, ColBERT-style late interaction, learned cross-encoder reranker.
    WhyDense embeddings catch semantically similar passages; BM25 kills the “semantic but lexically wrong” matches that dense-only retrieval is famous for. The reranker runs in <5 ms on the top-50 set.
    ReferenceRobertson (1994) — Some simple effective approximations to the 2-Poisson model for probabilistic weighted retrieval; Karpukhin et al. (2020) — Dense Passage Retrieval for Open-Domain Question Answering.

    10. Per-user LoRA over global personalisation

    DecisionEach user gets their own LoRA adapter, keyed by SHA-256 of their biometric template; never aggregated, never uploaded.
    AlternativesSingle global personalisation embedding, federated averaging across users, server-side per-user fine-tuning.
    WhyPrivacy by architecture: there is literally no aggregation surface. Biometric gating means a user’s adapter only loads when their typing pattern matches; impostors get the base model. Federated averaging is the natural next step (see roadmap), but only after DP noise injection on the LoRA deltas.
    ReferenceHu et al. (2021); McMahan et al. (2017) — Communication-Efficient Learning of Deep Networks from Decentralized Data (FedAvg, future work).

    11. Rule-based coreference over neural

    DecisionDeterministic recency-stack pronoun resolver. Each turn pushes its named entities; pronouns resolve to the most recent compatible entry.
    AlternativesNeural coref (e.g. SpanBERT-based), CoreNLP, end-to-end attention over the full dialogue history.
    WhyLatency: <1 ms vs. >50 ms for a small neural coref. Debuggability: the demo can show which rule fired. Adequate for the demo’s 2–3 entity, 5–10 turn horizon. The interface in i3/dialogue/coref.py is swappable for a learned model when long-range reference matters.
    ReferenceLappin & Leass (1994) — An Algorithm for Pronominal Anaphora Resolution; Mitkov (1998) — Robust Pronoun Resolution with Limited Knowledge.

    12. Char-CNN safety classifier over rule-based

    DecisionA 4-class char-level CNN (~48 k params) trained from scratch on a 2 000-example synthetic constitutional corpus, replacing the regex hostility filter on the inbound path.
    AlternativesPure regex / keyword filter, fine-tuning a pretrained classifier (Detoxify, Llama Guard), API-based moderation (OpenAI, Perspective).
    WhyRegex misses leetspeak / obfuscation and over-flags benign mentions; fine-tuned heads import HuggingFace machinery which violates the "from scratch" pitch; API moderation breaks the on-device privacy story. A char-level CNN sees raw bytes, learns n-gram patterns, ships in <200 KB, and exposes a refusal-reasoning surface (verdict, confidence, principle, suggested response) that the regex filter cannot.
    ReferenceBai et al. (2022) — Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback; Zhang, Zhao & LeCun (2015) — Character-level Convolutional Networks for Text Classification.

    13. Hybrid edge-cloud over edge-only

    DecisionLocal SLM is the default; cloud LLM is an opt-in fallback for high-complexity prompts, gated by user consent and a privacy budget.
    AlternativesEdge-only (no cloud at all), cloud-only, always-cloud-with-edge-fallback.
    WhyProduction realism: a 204 M model genuinely cannot match a foundation LLM on out-of-domain Q&A, but an honest HMI system shouldn’t hide that. The hybrid story shows engineering judgement on the JD’s “foundational LLMs” line: edge-first defaults, cloud as a privacy-budget-bounded escalation.
    ReferenceChen et al. (2023) — FrugalGPT (cost-quality tradeoff); the LinUCB router in i3/router/bandit.py generalises this to a learned policy.

    References

    Every paper, every file.

    A flat alphabetised bibliography of the works cited in the codebase. Each entry includes a one-paragraph summary of how it informed the implementation and a pointer to the file where it lives.

    Bai, Kolter & Koltun (2018) — An Empirical Evaluation of Generic Convolutional and Recurrent Networks for Sequence Modeling

    arXiv:1803.01271

    The paper that argued dilated TCNs match or exceed RNNs on a wide range of sequence tasks while being parallelisable and memory-efficient. Our 4-block dilated TCN encoder over the 32-dim sliding feature window is a direct application of the residual TCN block they propose.

    used in: i3/encoder/tcn.py, i3/encoder/blocks.py

    Bai et al. (2022) — Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback

    arXiv:2212.08073

    Anthropic’s framework for using a constitution-style prompt to drive RLAIF refusal calibration. We don’t train with this loop today, but the safety story in i3/safety/ follows the constitutional pattern of explicit principle-driven rejection, and it’s named in the roadmap as the next step for refusal tightening.

    used in: i3/safety/ (informal), docs/TECHNICAL_REPORT.md §8

    Caruana (1997) — Multitask Learning

    Machine Learning, 28(1)

    The canonical reference for auxiliary-task learning as a regulariser. Our v2 SLM has three optional auxiliary heads — typing biometrics, affect, reading-level — trained alongside the next-token loss. They’re off the inference path but on the training graph.

    used in: i3/slm/multi_task_heads.py

    Eyben, Wöllmer & Schuller (2010) — openSMILE: The Munich Versatile and Fast Open-Source Audio Feature Extractor

    ACM Multimedia 2010

    openSMILE’s feature catalogue defines the canonical low-level prosodic descriptors (pitch, jitter, shimmer, energy, spectral statistics). Our client-side prosody extractor reimplements a small subset of these features in JavaScript so audio never leaves the browser.

    used in: i3/multimodal/prosody.py, web/js/voice_prosody.js

    Flesch (1948); Gunning (1952)

    Foundational readability metrics

    Flesch-Kincaid Grade and Gunning-Fog Index are the two oldest readability metrics still in use. Our linguistic feature extractor computes both from scratch (no textstat dependency) along with type-token ratio, sentence length statistics, and emoji density.

    used in: i3/interaction/linguistic.py

    Graves (2016) — Adaptive Computation Time for Recurrent Neural Networks

    arXiv:1603.08983

    Introduced the per-step halting probability formulation we adapt to a transformer. Our ACT controller emits a halting probability per token at each layer; tokens with cumulative halting probability above threshold are frozen via a halting mask. The ponder loss is the original Graves formulation.

    used in: i3/slm/act_halting.py, i3/slm/aux_losses.py

    Houlsby et al. (2019) — Parameter-Efficient Transfer Learning for NLP

    ICML 2019

    Introduced adapter modules: small bottleneck layers inserted into each transformer layer that are the only thing trained. Our LoRA implementation is the rank-decomposed reformulation that came two years later, but the conceptual lineage is direct.

    used in: i3/personalisation/lora_adapter.py (informally cited)

    Howard et al. (2019) — Searching for MobileNetV3

    ICCV 2019

    MobileNetV3-Small is the visual backbone for our gaze classifier. Frozen weights, with a small per-session calibration head trained on 4 fixation targets. The ONNX export runs in-tab via onnxruntime-web so camera frames never leave the browser.

    used in: i3/multimodal/gaze_classifier.py, web/js/gaze_capture.js

    Hu et al. (2021) — LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models

    arXiv:2106.09685

    The reformulation of adapter modules as a low-rank update W ← W + BA. We use rank 8 on the attention projections, one adapter per biometric template, never aggregated. The whole personalisation story rides on this paper.

    used in: i3/personalisation/lora_adapter.py

    Killourhy & Maxion (2009) — Comparing Anomaly-Detection Algorithms for Keystroke Dynamics

    DSN 2009

    Provides the canonical benchmark for keystroke-dynamics features and the Mahalanobis-distance baseline that beats most learned alternatives at this task size. Our continuous-auth scorer is essentially their Mahalanobis classifier with a sliding-window template update.

    used in: i3/biometric/keystroke_auth.py, i3/biometric/continuous_auth.py

    Li, Chu, Langford & Schapire (2010) — A Contextual-Bandit Approach to Personalized News Article Recommendation

    WWW 2010

    Introduces LinUCB: linear regression with an analytical UCB confidence interval. Our edge/cloud router is a 12-dim LinUCB, with the cloud arm gated additionally by user consent and a privacy budget.

    used in: i3/router/bandit.py

    Madaan et al. (2023) — Self-Refine: Iterative Refinement with Self-Feedback

    arXiv:2303.17651

    Demonstrates that an LLM can critique and refine its own output. Our self-critique loop is a much smaller, single-pass version: the critic emits a tag from a closed set and, if non-none, regeneration runs once with the critique note prepended.

    used in: i3/critique/critic.py

    Monrose & Rubin (1997) — Authentication via Keystroke Dynamics

    CCS 1997

    The original paper that established keystroke dynamics as a viable continuous-authentication signal. Defined the dwell/flight feature schema we still use, plus the registration-plus-verification protocol that the Identity Lock pill in the nav implements.

    used in: i3/biometric/keystroke_auth.py

    Picard (1997) — Affective Computing

    MIT Press

    The book that founded the field of affective computing — the framing that emotional state is observable from physiological and behavioural signals and is a legitimate input to HMI systems. Our affect classifier and adaptation pathway are direct applications of this thesis.

    used in: i3/affect/state_classifier.py, i3/affect/shift_detector.py

    Robertson (1994) — Some Simple Effective Approximations to the 2-Poisson Model for Probabilistic Weighted Retrieval

    SIGIR 1994

    The BM25 paper. Our retrieval reranker is BM25 over the top-k=50 dense-cosine candidates — a hybrid that catches what dense-only retrieval misses (lexically-divergent semantic neighbours).

    used in: i3/slm/retrieval.py

    Schuller (2009) — The INTERSPEECH 2009 Emotion Challenge

    INTERSPEECH 2009

    Defined the canonical computational paralinguistics evaluation: the link between low-level prosodic features and affective state. Our prosody-driven contribution to the user-state embedding draws on this framing.

    used in: i3/multimodal/prosody.py, i3/multimodal/voice.py

    Sennrich, Haddow & Birch (2015) — Neural Machine Translation of Rare Words with Subword Units

    ACL 2016

    Introduced Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) for NMT. Our hand-written tokenizer is the byte-level variant popularised by GPT-2: 32 k vocab, 217 LOC, no sentencepiece or tokenizers Rust crate. Trained on the combined 974 k-pair dialogue corpus.

    used in: i3/slm/bpe_tokenizer.py

    Shazeer et al. (2017) — Outrageously Large Neural Networks: The Sparsely-Gated Mixture-of-Experts Layer

    ICLR 2017

    The MoE-FFN paper. Our 2-expert mixture-of-experts replaces the standard FFN at every transformer layer; the gate is a softmax over a learned linear projection of the 8-dim adaptation vector. The load-balance auxiliary loss is the original Shazeer formulation.

    used in: i3/slm/moe_ffn.py, i3/slm/aux_losses.py

    Sweller (1988) — Cognitive Load During Problem Solving

    Cognitive Science, 12(2)

    Cognitive load theory: working memory has a finite budget and the system designer’s job is to minimise extraneous load. Our cognitive_load adaptation axis is a direct mapping: when the user’s typing pattern signals high load (long IKI variance, edit bursts, slow composition), the post-processor tightens phrasing and the SLM is biased toward shorter sentences.

    used in: i3/adaptation/, i3/affect/state_classifier.py

    About

    A portfolio piece.

    Implicit Interaction Intelligence is an HMI demo built for the Huawei R&D UK internship application. Every component is hand-written, ships from a single repository, and is intentionally edge-deployable.

    Q1. Custom ML from scratch?
    SLM, tokenizer, TCN encoder, LinUCB bandit, linguistic feature extractors and keystroke analytics — all hand-written PyTorch.
    Q2. SLM without heavy frameworks?
    204 M-parameter MoE+ACT decoder transformer with per-layer cross-attention conditioning, byte-level BPE tokenizer (32 k vocab). Zero HuggingFace dependencies in the generation path.
    Q3. Pipeline orchestration?
    21-stage, wave-based DAG in scripts/run_everything.py. Reproduces the entire stack from a clean checkout in ~10 min.
    Q4. Edge deployment?
    ONNX export of the encoder (~0.4 MB), browser-side inference via WebGPU/WASM, edge-profile harness, byte/message/rate caps for wearable budgets.
    Built on a single 6 GB laptop GPU. Designed to be quantised to 8-bit and shipped to a phone.
